MicroVerse: Measuring Self-Authored Identity Drift in Multi-Agent Simulations

YH
Yuexing Hao
Core Team
📅 June 20, 2026
⏱ 11 min read

When you run a persona through a 10-turn conversation with another agent, does the persona stay consistent? Or does it slowly become a different character?

We found that LLM agents drift away from their assigned personas over time—especially in long-horizon interactions. This "identity drift" is a critical issue for simulation validity. If your persona agent morphs into someone else mid-simulation, your evaluation is invalid.

MicroVerse is a measurement toolkit and framework for detecting, quantifying, and correcting this drift.

The Problem: Identity Drift

Here's a concrete example. We simulated a conversation between two personas:

  • Alex: A tech-literate 28-year-old software engineer, risk-tolerant, likes trying new features
  • Pat: A 62-year-old retiree, risk-averse, prefers simple, familiar interfaces

The task: Have a 20-turn conversation about a new fintech app.

In turn 1, Alex behaves like a tech engineer (asks about APIs, mentions "blockchain") and Pat behaves like a retiree (asks about safety, mentions "my grandkids").

By turn 15, something strange happens: both agents have drifted toward similar, generic personas. They're making similar vocabulary choices, asking similar questions, even showing similar risk tolerance.

Pat suddenly says something like "I'm curious about the API architecture" — something completely out of character. Alex becomes overly cautious: "But is my money safe?"

This isn't random noise. It's systematic drift: the agents are converging toward a "middle ground" persona that belongs to neither character.

Why Does This Happen?

LLM agents don't have persistent memory of their persona instructions. Each turn, the prompt is reconstructed:

You are Pat, a 62-year-old retiree... --- [full conversation history] --- Your next response:

Over 20 turns, the conversation history grows much longer than the persona description. The LLM sees 10,000+ tokens of conversation vs. 200 tokens of persona instruction.

The persona instruction is diluted. The agent's behavior is increasingly shaped by the conversation's own dynamics—what Alex said, tone, vocabulary—rather than the persona's original traits.

Measuring Drift with MicroVerse

MicroVerse quantifies identity drift using three metrics:

1. Behavioral Consistency (BC): How much does the agent's decision-making deviate from its persona's decision heuristics over time? Scale: 0–100 (100 = no drift).
2. Linguistic Consistency (LC): How much does the agent's vocabulary, sentence structure, and tone diverge from its persona profile? Measured by comparing early-turn text embeddings to late-turn embeddings.
3. Persona Fidelity (PF): How well does the agent maintain its assigned persona when directly queried ("What's your age? Your job? Your risk tolerance?"). Measures explicit vs. implicit consistency.

In our fintech conversation example:

  • Pat's BC dropped from 94 (turn 1) to 68 (turn 20)
  • Pat's LC dropped from 92 to 71
  • Pat's PF stayed at 95 (persona memory is intact; behavior drifted anyway)

The key insight: Pat remembers being a retiree, but doesn't act like one anymore.

Causes of Drift

We identified four drift mechanisms:

  • Context dilution: Persona instructions fade as conversation history grows. Fix: Use attention mechanisms or explicit memory to prioritize early instructions.
  • Tone matching: The agent unconsciously mirrors the other agent's vocabulary and sentence structure. Fix: Enforce persona-specific vocabulary constraints in the prompt.
  • Convergence pressure: In dialogue, agents naturally converge toward shared understanding. This is good for coherence, bad for persona diversity. Fix: Explicitly penalize convergence toward the average.
  • Task-goal override: The agent prioritizes "succeed at the conversation task" over "stay in character." Fix: Reweight persona consistency as an explicit objective.

Correcting Drift: The MicroVerse Toolkit

MicroVerse provides three mitigation strategies:

Strategy 1: Persona Anchoring
Every N turns (e.g., every 5 turns), inject a persona reminder into the system prompt:
"You are [persona]. Remember: [key trait]. Your last response should have reflected this."
Result: BC stays above 85 for 20+ turns.
Strategy 2: Lexical Constraints
Enforce that the agent uses domain-specific vocabulary consistent with its persona. A 62-year-old retiree shouldn't say "blockchain." A software engineer should mention APIs.
Result: LC stays above 88.
Strategy 3: Adversarial Divergence
Add a "divergence loss" to the agent's objective: reward responses that maintain persona distinction from other agents.
Result: Agents maintain 15–20% higher BC in multi-agent scenarios.

Impact: Comparing Simulations

We ran the same fintech conversation study three ways:

  • Baseline: No drift correction. Results drifted significantly; hard to interpret.
  • With anchoring: Persona consistency maintained. Results interpretable but slightly artificial (agents sounded scripted).
  • With anchoring + divergence: Natural dialogue, maintained personas. Results matched real human conversations with 91% accuracy.

The third approach—our recommended best practice—treats persona consistency as a first-class objective alongside task success.

Research Questions MicroVerse Unlocks

Once you can measure drift, you can explore:

  • Do certain persona dimensions drift more than others? (We found risk tolerance drifts more than expertise.)
  • How does drift scale with conversation length? (Roughly linear: 1% BC loss per turn.)
  • Can you predict which persona pairs will drift toward each other? (Yes: homophily bias in embeddings predicts convergence.)
  • Does drift affect simulation validity? (Yes: if BC drops below 75, results diverge from real user behavior by >10%.)

Using MicroVerse
